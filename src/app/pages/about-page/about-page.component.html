Rubin Observatory and the Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST)

The Vera C. Rubin Observatory is a state-of-the-art astronomical facility designed to conduct the Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST), a ten-year survey that will capture an unprecedented view of the dynamic night sky. Equipped with an 8.4-meter telescope and the world’s largest digital camera, LSST will generate approximately 20 terabytes of data each night. This massive dataset will enable researchers to study transient phenomena, track near-Earth objects, probe dark matter and dark energy, and explore the evolving structure of the universe.

LSST will help scientists answer some of the biggest questions about our universe. By tracking asteroids and comets, it will improve our ability to detect objects that might come close to Earth. By observing how galaxies change over time, LSST will help us understand the mysterious forces shaping the cosmos, such as dark energy. The survey will also allow astronomers to see supernova explosions and other dramatic cosmic events as they happen, providing new insights into the life cycles of stars and the nature of space itself.

The Pitt Google Broker

The Pitt Google Broker is a powerful event broker designed to process and distribute the vast amount of astronomical data produced by the LSST. Hosted by the University of Pittsburgh, this broker leverages Google Cloud’s scalable infrastructure to rapidly filter, classify, and disseminate real-time alerts about celestial events. By providing researchers with access to timely and curated data, the Pitt Google Broker enhances scientific discovery and enables astronomers worldwide to efficiently identify and study transient events in the universe.

What is Google Cloud?

Google Cloud is a suite of cloud computing services that provides scalable storage, computing power, and machine learning capabilities. By leveraging Google Cloud’s robust infrastructure, the Pitt Google Broker is able to process the immense volume of LSST data efficiently, ensuring rapid analysis and dissemination of astronomical alerts. With tools such as BigQuery for data analysis, Cloud Functions for real-time processing, and AI-driven analytics, Google Cloud empowers astronomers to unlock new insights from LSST observations.
